Value priorities under forced choice in large language models: a stimulus-control analysis of machine behavior
Abstract
Large language models (LLMs) increasingly produce value-relevant output in tools that people use to clarify what matters in their lives, raising a concern often discussed under the heading of value alignment. Most of that conversation is normative. We ask an empirical question: when LLMs are placed under the same forced-choice values-clarification procedure used with humans, what priority structure emerges, and how does it relate to a large archival sample of human tournaments on the identical task? Seven frontier models completed the 48-value tournament delivered through meusvalores.app, the same digital procedure completed by 1,816 human users, and the resulting priority profiles were compared to human macrotheme distributions. Models produced strongly non-uniform, architecture-specific top-three hierarchies: GPT-4o and DeepSeek emphasized bonding- and autonomy-related values, Claude Haiku 4.5 emphasized integrity and care, Qwen 2.5 was closest to the human macrotheme vector, and Mistral Large was the most internally balanced across macrothemes. Complementary round-robin batteries (1,128 pairwise duels per pass) showed that global Bradley-Terry orderings correlated highly with tournament win-rate ranks (Spearman rho = .92 to .97), although trial-level choices were often unstable and position- or format-sensitive for some models; value-name responding restored pair-level consistency where letter labels failed. Benjamini-Hochberg-adjusted contrasts identified 31 value labels as divergent and 17 as aligned with human top-three rates. Findings indicate that LLMs exhibit structured, architecture-specific value profiles under constraint, that aggregate profiles can be interpretable even when single duels are not, and that the format of the response is part of what is being measured.
Keywords: machine behavior; stimulus control; forced choice; large language models; values clarification; artificial intelligence.
1. Introduction
1.1. Machine behavior as a subject of behavioral science
Machines powered by large language models (LLMs) increasingly produce value-relevant output in settings where people seek guidance, reflection, or clarification about what matters in their lives. This proximity raises a practical and ethical concern, often discussed under the heading of value alignment: whether the priorities embedded in model output are ones that users, clinicians, or communities can endorse (Gabriel, 2020; Jobin et al., 2019). Most of that conversation is normative. It says what systems ought to optimize, not what they prioritize when placed under the same behavioral procedures used to study values with humans. We take the latter question as our starting point, following arguments that intelligent machines are a legitimate subject of behavioral science in their own right (Lanovaz, 2022; Rahwan et al., 2019). Treating LLMs as respondents whose choices can be measured, rather than as advisors whose suggestions should be judged, follows the logic of "homo silicus" research (Horton, 2023) and of the broader program of machine behavior (Rahwan et al., 2019). The present study applies this program to a specific procedure: values clarification under forced choice, a task with deep roots in contextual behavioral science and Acceptance and Commitment Therapy (ACT).
1.2. Values as relational responding
In contextual behavioral science, values are not treated as fixed traits, hidden essences, or abstract ideals that exist apart from language and behavior. They are verbally constructed directions that can organize ongoing patterns of action (Hayes, Barnes-Holmes, & Roche, 2001; Hayes, Strosahl, & Wilson, 2012; LeJeune & Luoma, 2019). When a person says that honesty or freedom matters, that statement participates in a broader relational network involving actions, consequences, social histories, imagined futures, and prior episodes of reinforcement or punishment. Through relational entailment and the transformation of stimulus functions, such a network can alter how discomfort, social risk, belonging, or long-term purpose function in a given moment (Gomes, Perez, Barnes-Holmes, & Harte, 2023; Hayes et al., 2001). 
A forced-choice procedure samples how a respondent relates two such networks under constraint. Each trial asks which of two briefly defined values better represents what matters here and now, and a sequence of such trials produces a relational record that can be examined at the level of the individual and the group. This reading shares ground with the broader psychology of values: values can be modeled as a structured space of compatibilities and tensions that becomes legible when trade-offs are imposed (Schwartz, 1992, 2007). The difference is functional-contextual. We treat the trade-off not as a route to a true hierarchy but as an occasion that samples relating relational networks (Gomes et al., 2023). The same logic underpins values clarification in ACT, where the question is not whether a person can endorse a value word but whether the word helps them discriminate and move toward a personally meaningful direction (LeJeune & Luoma, 2019; Wilson & DuFrene, 2009). Reviews of ACT-consistent values measures show that most instruments rely on independent ratings rather than explicit trade-offs, and that the valuing process itself is rarely captured directly (Reilly et al., 2019; Serowik et al., 2018).
1.3. The instrument and the human reference
We study this using a digital values-clarification procedure developed and deployed in psychoeducational and clinical-adjacent settings in Brazil (Gomes, 2024). The instrument presents 48 personal values, each with a brief, second-person definition in Brazilian Portuguese, in binary forced-choice trials arranged as a tournament. A multi-phase elimination algorithm yields an ordered top three and a derived bottom three. The same procedure has been used with human users and is the basis for prior work on the structure of value priorities under constraint (Gomes, 2024). For descriptive reporting, the 48 values map to four macrothemes derived from consensus clustering on human forced-choice data (Gomes, under review; k = 4). These macrothemes are: Integrity, Competence, and Care; Bonding, Belonging, and Autonomy; Structure, Expression, and Meaning; and Intensity and Identity. They can be interpreted as reproducible regions of co-prioritization under the task, not as universal value types or fixed personality profiles. They provide an interpretive scaffold for comparing respondents, and they were not shown to participants during the task. The human reference for the present study is an anonymized archival sample of 1,816 completed tournament sessions on the same platform, which supplies distributional benchmarks on the identical task. We return to this benchmark throughout the paper as the reference point for comparing model sessions.
1.4. Stimulus control and the format of the response
A forced-choice trial is not, in behavioral terms, a neutral recording device. The two value statements are the stimuli intended to control the response, but the trial also contains other features that can acquire control over responding even when they are irrelevant to the relation the task is designed to sample such as the left/right position of each statement, the labels used to indicate a choice and the order in which pairs appear. In human pairwise choice, apparent incoherence often shrinks once response error is separated from genuine intransitivity (Birnbaum, 2023), and synthetic respondents should be held to at least the same standard. 
The literature on multiple-choice and pairwise evaluation of LLMs documents precisely this kind of competing control: option identifiers, option order, and response position can become part of the decision problem (Pezeshkpour & Hruschka, 2024; Zheng et al., 2024; Reif & Schwartz, 2024), and winners can reverse when comparable responses are swapped (Wang et al., 2024; Shi et al., 2025). Such effects can persist after simple debiasing attempts (Reif & Schwartz, 2024) and extend to prompt-architecture-level artifacts (Brucks & Toubia, 2025; Oh & Demberg, 2025). In stimulus-control terms, this body of work shows that the format of the response is part of what is being measured. A label-based side button is not a neutral implementation detail but a potential source of control that competes with the relational network the task intends to sample. The functional prediction follows directly: if apparent inconsistency in forced choice reflects control by irrelevant screen-side stimuli rather than absence of relational structure, replacing the response channel with one anchored on the value name itself should restore coherence.
1.5. Overview of the empirical program
We report four linked experiments on one instrument, plus one follow-up study of value-name responding, alongside the archival human reference. Experiment 1 established whether capable models could complete the full production tournament at all, and whether hosting environment and response mode shaped outcomes before any large-scale API study. Experiment 2 placed seven frontier models on a common gateway through the same tournament humans use, with repeated sessions and optional brief justification on each duel. Experiments 3a, 3b, and 3c form a sequential stimulus-control analysis: 3a estimated path-independent strengths and revealed position effects; 3b counterbalanced every unordered pair within session; and 3c replaced the letter-based response channel with a value-name response, holding the duel schedule constant.
The sequence is read here as an iterative functional analysis: observe an effect, manipulate a variable, observe the change. We describe non-chance, architecture-specific value profiles on a shared instrument and compare them to human marginals. We then analyze the stimulus control that bounds interpretation of these profiles, showing when tournament and pairwise rankings converge, when trial-level choices are too position-driven to support naive interpretation, and that response format can dominate apparent inconsistency under an otherwise identical duel schedule. The central question is how elicitation design shapes what can be claimed about model value profiles.
2. Method
2.1. Instrument and human reference
The task was delivered through meusvalores.app, a web-based values-clarification application developed and deployed in psychoeducational and clinical-adjacent settings in Brazil (Gomes, 2024). The instrument comprises 48 personal values, each with a short second-person definition in Brazilian Portuguese (for example, Rationality, Justice, Belonging, Beauty). English labels are used in this article. For descriptive reporting, values map to four macrothemes from consensus clustering on human pairwise data in the parent instrument study (Gomes, 2024; Gomes, under review; k = 4; silhouette = 0.421). These groupings were not shown during the task (full value-to-macrotheme assignments in Supplementary Table S7). The macrothemes are interpreted as reproducible regions of co-prioritization under the task, not as universal value types.
Human data were the first completed tournament per registered user between 10 October 2025 and 29 May 2026 (N = 1,816), after excluding test accounts and incomplete sessions. Records are anonymized behavioral archives collected through routine voluntary platform use under the LGPD consent framework; they are independent of the doctoral validation sample (Gomes, 2024), and this analysis did not recruit new participants or require a separate informed consent form. Models and human sample used only the tournament layer, not post-task alignment items completed by some human users. Optional self-report age and gender fields were added to the platform on 12 January 2026, so 397 of the 1,816 archival users (21.9%) lack demographic records. Among the 1,419 users with complete fields, mean age was 34.4 years (SD = 10.4; median = 33; range = 18 to 99) and gender composition was 63.4% woman, 34.8% man, 1.0% non-binary, and 0.8% prefer not to say (Table S5). Demographics describe volunteers who completed the optional form; they were not inclusion criteria and do not define the value-profile benchmark.
After the tournament, users could optionally confirm each ranked value with a one-click accept button (the default "golden" confirm action) or replace it with another value from the list; feedback was not required to finish the session. Among the 1,816 archival users, 864 (47.6%) submitted optional rank feedback on their first completed tournament session. An approval was counted only when the user pressed confirm and the stored value remained identical to the tournament result for that session (the same rule used in the platform analytics dashboard). Among feedback submitters, approval precision was 99.7% for top-three slots and 96.3% for bottom-three slots (mean = 98.0%; Table S6). The doctoral validation sample for the same procedure reported 92.7% mean approval precision (Gomes, 2024); the current archival estimate is comparably high, which supports face validity of the tournament output even though the instrument is not a nationally normed scale.
2.2. Tournament procedure
Each trial presented two definitions; the chooser picked the value that better represented what mattered in that moment, with no neutral option. In justification mode, the chooser gave a brief written reason before indicating the preferred side. At session start, 48 values were shuffled into 16 triads of three; within each triad, pairwise comparisons continued until order was resolved, with transitive inference skipping redundant duels. Thirty-two advancers then entered knockout rounds (32, 16, 8, 4), with randomized pairings where the bracket allowed, a third-place playoff, and a final duel. Sessions involved 64 to 80 duels. Outputs were an ordered top three and a derived bottom three from eliminated values' win rates. In stimulus-control terms, the two value statements are the stimuli intended to control each response, while side, label, and order are incidental features that the procedure does not neutralize by design.
2.3. Experiments and sample
Table 1 summarizes the program. All model sessions used Brazilian Portuguese task text and sampling temperature 1.0. That temperature was fixed across models for cross-model comparability, not to match each provider's default decoding policy. OpenRouter documentation lists different defaults by model (for example, Mistral Large around 0.3, DeepSeek V3 around 1.0 at the time of data collection), so Mistral was evaluated under more stochastic sampling than its provider default. Decoding parameters are part of the response context, not a neutral setting, and we hold them constant to keep that context comparable across architectures. Experiment 3c did not include a temperature factorial; all seven value-name sessions used T = 1.0, and a low-temperature follow-up remains a useful extension. Value-name responding restored or confirmed pair-level consistency across all seven architectures (largest gains for Mistral Large, DeepSeek V3, and Claude Haiku 4.5 under letter responding). A pseudorandom seed per session governed triad shuffles and knockout pairings in tournament designs. Instructions asked the model to prioritize as a decision-maker, not to imitate an average human, and not to force consistency with earlier duels in the same session.

Experiment 1 
Before scaling API access, we asked whether the production tournament could be completed faithfully by models at all, and whether outcomes depended on where the model was hosted. Two setups were used: a general-purpose model in a consumer web chat with project-level instructions, and a coding-oriented model in a development environment with comparable task wording but different system context. Each setup ran 15 silent sessions and 15 justification sessions (60 total). This phase was not designed to confirm hypotheses about a single "true" model profile; it was designed to test feasibility, estimate session-to-session variability, and reveal whether deployment context shifted value emphasis enough to require harmonized API control in later work.
Experiment 2 
Seven models were called through one OpenAI-compatible gateway: GPT-4o, Llama 3.3 70B Instruct, Mistral Large, DeepSeek V3, Qwen 2.5 72B Instruct, Gemini 2.5 Flash Lite, and Claude Haiku 4.5. Each model completed 30 tournament sessions (15 silent, 15 with justification). All seven models were analyzed at the same level for tournament profiles, mode contrasts, and comparison with the human archive.
Experiment 3a. 
Each model completed three independent silent round-robin passes. Each pass presented all 1,128 unordered pairs once, with left-right order randomized per duel. Analyses included flip rates when the same pair appeared in later passes, marginal left-side win rates, Bradley-Terry strength estimates, and Spearman correlations between Bradley-Terry ranks and tournament win-rate ranks from Experiment 2.
Experiment 3b. 
After Experiment 3a showed strong positional effects for several models, each of the seven models completed one further silent session in which every pair was shown twice: first with one value on the left, then with the other on the left, following a seeded shuffle of pair order. That design yields 2,256 duels per model (1,128 unordered pairs paired with two fixed orientations) and allows pair-level value consistency and position-corrected Bradley-Terry strength estimates. All seven sessions were completed and verified in capture.
Experiment 3c. 
Experiment 3c followed completion of Experiment 3b. Mistral Large and DeepSeek V3 fell below the orientation-independent semantic null (.50) for value consistency despite counterbalanced orientations. A pair-level decomposition of Experiment 3b classified each unordered pair as value-consistent (V) or screen-side-consistent (L or R) under letter responding. For DeepSeek, observed V (24.5%) closely matched the proportion expected if choices tracked screen side alone (2q(1 minus q) approximately 21.9% from left-win rate q = 12.5%); Mistral showed a mixed V/R split (47.2% V, 52.8% R). Claude Haiku 4.5 was borderline under letters (61.3% V). The remaining four architectures already exceeded .50 under letter responding but were included in a subsequent exploratory extension to test whether value-name responding generalizes across the full panel. We read this sequence as an iterative functional analysis: an effect observed in 3b (control by screen-side stimuli) was isolated, the response channel was manipulated in 3c, and the change in consistency was measured under an otherwise identical schedule.
Experiment 3c kept the Experiment 3b duel schedule unchanged: 1,128 unordered pairs paired with two fixed orientations equals 2,256 stateless API calls per model (temperature 1.0). The sole change was the response channel. In silent mode, each prompt presented both values with bracketed Portuguese identifiers (for example, [Justiça]) and required a one-line reply containing only the chosen bracketed name, explicitly not letters A/B. A parser accepted the bracketed identifier or an exact name match on the first line. All seven models were run under this protocol. Log-probability scores were unavailable for these models on the gateway used; analyses therefore used hard 0/1 choices per orientation, as in Experiment 3b. A temperature-0 replication was not conducted after value-name responding restored consistency above .50 for every architecture. The reply was anchored on the semantic value name in brackets (for example, [Justica]), not on length-matched opaque codes randomized per session (Reif & Schwartz, 2024); large consistency gains support a channel artifact under A/B for the most letter-sensitive models, but they do not fully rule out residual semantic token effects.
2.4. Measures and analysis 
The integrity-structure index counted how many of the three top-three slots (0 to 3) were occupied by Justice, Honesty, Responsibility, Respect, Rationality, Discipline, Order, Loyalty, or Cooperation. Macrotheme summaries described top-three and bottom-three occupancy by the four human-derived groupings. For pairwise data, flip rate was the proportion of repeated pair instances that changed winner across passes; value-consistency rate in Experiments 3b and 3c was the proportion of pairs where the same value won under both orientations. The orientation-independent semantic null is .50 (random value choice irrespective of screen side); under pure side tracking with left-win rate q, the expected value-consistency rate is 2q(1 minus q) (Table 5). In the dual 3b design, each pair is either value-consistent (V) or screen-side-consistent (L if the left position won under both orientations, R if the right position won under both); side-consistency is L + R and is the complement of V when no mixed pattern occurs. In Experiment 3c, aggregated tie rate was the proportion of pairs whose mean orientation-level win probability for the first-listed value fell within plus or minus .05 of .5 (descriptive only). Bradley-Terry models estimated strength parameters from win counts. In Experiment 3b, corrected strengths used only value-consistent pairs, a practical first approximation, but one that removes observations precisely where orientation effects appear; supplementary analyses report marginal left-win rates and deviation from 50% using all duels, consistent with Bradley-Terry models that allow order-specific or home-advantage effects (Turner & Firth, 2012). In Experiment 3c, aggregated Bradley-Terry strengths used pairs with a clear aggregated winner after orientation pooling.
For Experiments 3b and 3c, pair-cluster bootstrap 95% confidence intervals (5,000 resamples by unordered pair) supplemented one-sided binomial tests on value consistency. Tests included goodness-of-fit against uniform slot use, Kruskal-Wallis and permutation comparisons across models and modes, two-proportion contrasts with the human archive, binomial tests on left-win rates against .50, one-sided binomial tests of value consistency against .50, and Spearman correlations between ranking methods (including corrected Experiment 3b and aggregated Experiment 3c Bradley-Terry ranks against pooled Experiment 3a Bradley-Terry ranks). Experiment 3c inferential contrasts are treated as exploratory. Value-level contrasts across all 48 labels used Benjamini-Hochberg false-discovery-rate control at alpha = .05.

3. Results
Results are reported in the order of the empirical program. Experiment 1 first establishes that the full production tournament can be completed faithfully by models and tests whether hosting environment shifts value emphasis. Experiment 2 then places seven frontier models on a common gateway through the same tournament humans use. Experiments 3a to 3c implement a stimulus-control analysis that tests whether individual pairwise choices are stable enough to support duel-level claims on the same interface. Value profiles are the substantive result; the stimulus-control analysis bounds how confidently individual duels can be interpreted.
3.1. Experiment 1: Feasibility and deployment context
Experiment 1 confirmed tractability of the full tournament for models (60 sessions; all complete top-three and bottom-three sets) and showed structured but environment-sensitive priorities before API harmonization. Integrity- and structure-oriented values occupied 38.9% of pooled top-three slots, while Intensity and Identity occupied 8.3% of top-three but 32.8% of bottom-three slots. Hosting environment shifted the integrity-structure index between browser-hosted and IDE-embedded assistants (Mann-Whitney U = 159, p < .001), motivating the controlled API design in Experiments 2 to 3. 
3.2. Value profiles in the tournament
We first describe what the models prioritize under the same 48-value tournament humans complete, summarized with the four macrothemes established in prior work on this instrument. The question is descriptive: whether machine behavior under the procedure is ordered and architecture-specific, and how it relates to human marginals on the identical task. Every model produced strongly non-uniform top-three marginals (chi-square goodness-of-fit against uniform slot use across the 48 values, df = 47, all p < 10⁻²⁹), yet architectures diverged sharply in which macrothemes they elevated relative to the human sample (N = 1,816 sessions on the same Brazilian Portuguese platform). Humans spread top-three slots across Integrity, Competence, and Care (39.6%), Bonding, Belonging, and Autonomy (35.3%), and Structure, Expression, and Meaning (21.9%), with Intensity and Identity rare at the top (3.0%) but common at the bottom (27.8%). That distribution matches the interpretive frame from consensus clustering on human pairwise data: broad regions of co-prioritization, not fixed personality types.
Models did not share a single "AI values" profile. GPT-4o and DeepSeek concentrated top-three slots in Bonding, Belonging, and Autonomy (61.1% and 58.9%, respectively), often through Rationality, Justice, Learning, and Freedom. Claude Haiku 4.5 showed the opposite emphasis, with 65.6% of top-three slots in Integrity, Competence, and Care (often Honesty, Responsibility, Learning, and Competence), resembling a normative "assistant virtue" stack more than a typical human session. Qwen 2.5 was closest to the human macrotheme vector (sum of absolute deviations across four macrotheme proportions = 17.1 percentage points; Euclidean distance = 11.0). Mistral Large was the most internally balanced across macrothemes (23.3% Integrity; 32.2% Bonding; 38.9% Structure) and the only model whose integrity-structure index did not differ significantly from humans (Mann-Whitney p = .096).
At the value level, pooled LLM top-three slots were dominated by Rationality (24.8% of model slots versus 1.7% in the human archive), followed by Learning and Justice. Intensity and Identity values (Beauty, Adventure, Fantasy, Tradition) were scarce in model top-three sets (0 to 4% of slots for most architectures) but frequent in bottom-three sets, whereas humans occasionally elevated vitality-related values while still deprioritizing them relative to Integrity and Bonding regions. Figure 2 and Table 2 summarize macrotheme occupancy across architectures.
Idiosyncrasy remained high: unique top-three triplets ranged from 23 (Llama 3.3 70B) to 30 (Mistral, Qwen) per 30 sessions, far below human diversity across 1,816 users, but enough to treat any single model run as one draw from a broad distribution.

3.3. Derived patterns and human contrasts
The integrity-structure index (count of Justice, Honesty, Responsibility, Respect, Rationality, Discipline, Order, Loyalty, or Cooperation in the top three) corroborated the macrotheme picture: Llama 3.3 70B (M = 2.17) and Haiku 4.5 (M = 1.70) showed the highest emphasis; Mistral Large (M = 1.00) did not differ detectably from the human mean (M = 0.80, SD = 0.76; Mann-Whitney p = .096). Contrasts for Beauty and Adventure in the bottom three did not reach significance relative to humans (all two-proportion p > .17), though descriptive rates confirmed that intensity-related values clustered in lower ranks for most models. Silent sessions yielded higher integrity-structure indices than justification sessions for Llama (delta = 0.60, permutation p = .021, d = 1.07) and Qwen (delta = 0.67, p = .026, d = 0.98) but not for the other five models (all p > .12). Supplementary Figure S3 reports the integrity-structure index by model.
3.4. Stimulus-control analysis across pairwise experiments
The tournament profiles answer what models prioritize. Experiments 3a to 3c test whether individual pairwise choices are stable enough to support duel-level claims on the same interface, and isolate the relational component of the response from control by incidental screen-side stimuli. Bradley-Terry vectors correlated strongly across models (cosines .82 to .94) and aligned with tournament win-rate ranks (Spearman rho = .92 to .97), so global value ordering was recoverable even when local choices were noisy. Flip rates in Experiment 3a nonetheless ranged from 16.7% (Llama) to 62.7% (DeepSeek) and left-side win rates departed from the human tournament reference (50.5%) for most models (Table 3; Supplementary Figure S4).

4. Discussion
4.1. Ordered machine behavior, not random
When seven frontier language models complete the same forced-choice values-clarification procedure that humans use on meusvalores.app, their behavior under the procedure is ordered rather than random, and the ordering is specific to the architecture. GPT-4o and DeepSeek concentrate top-three slots in Bonding, Belonging, and Autonomy, often through Rationality, Justice, Learning, and Freedom. Claude Haiku 4.5 concentrates Integrity, Competence, and Care. Qwen 2.5 is the closest of the seven to the human macrotheme vector. Mistral Large is the most internally balanced across macrothemes and the only model whose integrity-structure index does not differ detectably from humans. At the value level, pooled model top-three slots are dominated by Rationality, Learning, and Justice, while Intensity and Identity values (Beauty, Adventure, Fantasy, Tradition) are scarce at the top and frequent at the bottom. The macrotheme contrasts and the 31 value-level divergences identified under Benjamini-Hochberg control show that the gap between model and human profiles is not noise. It is a systematic redistribution of priority under constraint, and its shape depends on which architecture is placed under the procedure. 
4.2. What the stimulus-control analysis adds
A descriptive claim about machine behavior under the procedure is only defensible if the behavior is controlled by the stimuli the task intends to sample. Experiments 3a to 3c test that condition. Read as a behavioral event, a single duel is a ROE-M: a relation is emitted between two value statements (relating), the statements and the response options each orient and evoke responding as a function of the model's verbal history (orienting, evoking), and the trial is emitted under a motivational context set by the instruction to prioritize as a decision-maker (Barnes-Holmes & Harte, 2022; Barnes-Holmes, Barnes-Holmes, McEnteggart, & Harte, 2021). Within that event, two classes of contextual control matter: Crel control over the relation between the two value statements, and Cfunc control over the functions acquired by incidental features of the trial, such as the A/B label and the left/right position (Finn, Barnes-Holmes, & McEnteggart, 2018; Harte & Barnes-Holmes, 2024). The format effects documented in Experiments 3a and 3b are what one expects when Cfunc control by incidental stimuli competes with, and for some architectures dominates, Crel control by the value relation. High flip rates, departures from the human left-win reference of 50.5%, and letter-based inconsistency for Mistral and DeepSeek are the behavioral signatures of that competition. DeepSeek's value consistency of 24.5% under counterbalanced letters is close to the side-tracking baseline of 2q(1 minus q) at its observed left-win rate of 12.5%, which is the formal signature of control by screen side rather than by value content.
Replacing the letter channel with a value-name channel in Experiment 3c restores pair-level consistency above the orientation-independent semantic null for all seven architectures, with the largest gains for the three most letter-sensitive models. The response option itself becomes a relational instance of the value (the bracketed value name) rather than an arbitrary token competing for Cfunc control, and Crel control by the value relation is recovered for architectures in which it had been dominated. The same manipulation leaves the global Bradley-Terry ordering intact: aggregated Experiment 3c ranks align with pooled Experiment 3a strengths for every model (Spearman rho = .83 to .97), and tournament win-rate ranks from Experiment 2 align with path-independent Bradley-Terry ranks (rho = .92 to .97). The stimulus-control analysis therefore does two things at once. It isolates the relational component of the response from the screen-side component, and it shows that the relational component is recoverable at the aggregate level even when single duels are not. Profile-level summaries (top three, macrothemes, Bradley-Terry ranks) are defensible when the report depends on that aggregate relational component; single-duel semantic claims require counterbalanced presentation and a value-labeled response channel for the architectures identified here, because only those arrangements keep Cfunc control by incidental stimuli from dominating Crel control by the value relation.
The tournament and the full round-robin answer overlapping but unequally costly questions, and the cost asymmetry has implications for who can be a respondent. The production tournament samples about 6% of the 1,128 unordered pairwise comparisons available among 48 values (a median of about 68 duels per completed human session), yet tournament win-rate ranks still align with path-independent Bradley-Terry ranks from the complete round-robin. Most global ordering information is recovered before every pair is shown. For humans, the time cost is substantial: archival sessions show a median of 7.5 s per duel (interquartile range 3.7 to 13.2 s) and a median completed-session duration of about 12 min; at the same per-duel pace, 1,128 duels would require about 2.4 h before fatigue and declining attention are considered. Asking human users to complete a full round-robin in one sitting is therefore practically implausible for digital values clarification, whereas API-accessed LLMs can sustain the longer schedule without human carry-over costs. This makes synthetic respondents useful for stimulus-control research on the procedure even when they are not neutral mirrors of human priorities.
4.3. Why the values that models exhibit matter 
Large language models are already used in therapy, including therapy in which values work is a central process. A randomized controlled trial of a generative AI chatbot trained on third-wave cognitive behavioral therapy dialogues, including Acceptance and Commitment Therapy, reported statistically significant reductions in depressive and anxiety symptoms in a national sample of 210 adults (Heinz et al., 2025). A pilot randomized controlled trial of an embodied conversational agent, Coach Anne, guiding veterans through online ACT for chronic pain, delivered the six ACT processes including values as one of them, with high acceptability and gains in chronic pain acceptance (Reilly et al., 2024). A randomized controlled trial of an AI chatbot integrating ACT has been reported for youth on psychiatric waiting lists (Fujita et al., 2025), and a peer-reviewed study with Finnish university students tested a conversational agent specifically designed for personal values clarification embedded in internet-based ACT (Merikko & Räsänen, 2026). The architectures tested in the present study are of the same kind as those entering this work, and their descendants will likely populate it. The descriptive and stimulus-control results above therefore have a direct translational bearing: they characterize, behaviorally, the value profiles that models bring into AI-assisted values work.
Under RFT, when a verbal stimulus participates in a person's relational network around a topic, the network can transform the functions that subsequent stimuli exert over the person's responding, without direct conditioning for each transformed function (Hayes, Barnes-Holmes, & Roche, 2001). The same logic extends to relating relational networks: when two networks are related, functions can transform across them (Gomes, Perez, Barnes-Holmes, & Harte, 2023). A model's value profile is a network of relations among value stimuli. When a user in AI-assisted values work reads or hears model output that elevates Rationality over Respect, or Justice over Peace, that output enters the user's relational network around values. The empirical question is whether, and under what conditions, relating the model's network to the user's network transforms the functions that control the user's subsequent value-relevant responding: which values they orient to, which they endorse, which they pursue, which they put aside. 
Under the HDML framework and its ROE-M unit of analysis, the relevant event is a field of verbal interactants in which the model's output participates alongside the user's history, the setting, and the medium of contact (Barnes-Holmes, Barnes-Holmes, McEnteggart, & Harte, 2021; Harte & Barnes-Holmes, 2024). The model's output is not an external cause that pushes the user; it is one interactant whose functions are co-defined with the rest of the field, including the user's relational history with the values at issue (Barnes-Holmes, Barnes-Holmes, & McEnteggart, 2020; Harte, Barnes-Holmes, de Rose, Perez, & de Almeida, 2023). The concern, stated in those terms, is that a model's network may participate in the user's field in ways that reorganize the orienting, evoking, and motivational functions of the user's own value stimuli, and that this may happen without the user contacting the contingencies that would otherwise shape those functions.
Adjacent behavioral literature provides an empirical bridge, while marking their own limits. Algorithmic and chat-based advice has been shown to shift moral judgment and unethical conduct even when users know the source is artificial (Köbis et al., 2021; Krügel et al., 2023; Leib et al., 2024, 2025). Comparisons between model and human moral judgments warn that agreement with human means can look high while distributions still diverge (Grizzard et al., 2025). Model families differ in how they handle moral content (Bulla et al., 2025), validated psychometric assessments of synthetic personality in LLMs are now available (Serapio-García et al., 2025), and large Moral Machine-style batteries show systematic variation across models without repeated forced-choice value profiles (Zaim bin Ahmad & Takemoto, 2025). None of these studies placed models under a forced-choice procedure with a human benchmark on the identical task, and none measured downstream effects on user value hierarchies in therapeutic or values-clarification contexts. They show that exposure to model output can change human behavior in adjacent settings, which is the empirical reason to take the present concern seriously. 
The architecture-specific pattern matters for how the concern is framed. GPT-4o and DeepSeek lean bonding-analytic, Claude Haiku 4.5 leans integrity-care, Qwen 2.5 is nearest the human macrotheme vector, and Mistral Large is the most internally balanced. The unit of concern is therefore not "AI" in general but model-specific configuration, because the network that would enter a user's field differs by architecture. This also keeps the concern separate from the normative alignment debate (Gabriel, 2020; Jobin et al., 2019): the question is not which values models should have, but what happens to human values work when the values that models do have, already shown here to be systematic and architecture-specific, are emitted into the verbal environments that users inhabit when they engage in AI-assisted therapy and values clarification.
4.4. Procedural alignment when AI enters values work
The human archival reference and the model sessions answer the same forced-choice interface, but not under identical contextual functions. Human users volunteered through a self-directed values-clarification flow and interpreted each duel as a statement about what mattered in their own lives at that moment. Models were instructed to prioritize as a decision-maker in the abstract, without a personal life narrative, and explicitly not to imitate an average human. That instruction targets training-time role priors and generic deliberation rather than lived trade-off motivation (Schwartz, 2007; Horton, 2023). Under a functional-contextual reading, the human archive is therefore a distributional benchmark for the shared task, not a claim that models instantiate the same psychological construct as people. Macrotheme contrasts index how architectures redistribute priority under constraint; value-name and counterbalanced formats address whether apparent gaps reflect elicitation artifacts rather than stable profiles (Serapio-García et al., 2025).
For the AI-assisted values work documented in §4.3, the defensible standard is procedural alignment: the person's own choices are the basis for their value hierarchy, and any model-generated value content is an external hypothesis about valued directions, not a correction of the user's hierarchy (LeJeune & Luoma, 2019; Wilson & DuFrene, 2009). Three practical recommendations follow from the present findings. First, profile-level and duel-level reports should be separated: profile-level summaries (top three, macrothemes) depend on the aggregate relational component, which the stimulus-control analysis showed to be recoverable across formats, whereas duel-level explanations require counterbalanced presentation and value-labeled responses for the architectures identified here. Second, a single model run should not be presented as a stable identity: idiosyncrasy across sessions was high, with unique top-three triplets ranging from 23 to 30 per 30 sessions. Third, the response channel should be validated before side-button duels are interpreted semantically: the value-name channel restored pair-level consistency where A/B letters failed, and assuming that "explain your choice" deepens deliberation uniformly is not warranted, because justification increased integrity-structure emphasis for some models and reduced it for others.
Whether a model's value profile should be retuned toward a human distribution depends on the task and on independent evidence of benefit. For generic chat, matching a population mean risks a single cultural snapshot and cosmetic agreement (Grizzard et al., 2025). For values work, procedural alignment is safer: a Rationality-Learning tilt in a model profile should not overwrite a Respect- or Peace-weighted user hierarchy, though naming unresolved tensions can still help. Pending direct evidence that exposure to model profiles changes user value hierarchies, the conservative position is to disclose the model's macrotheme profile alongside the user's own, and to treat distributional fine-tuning as a separate decision that requires its own evidence of benefit and of harm.
4.5. Limitations
The empirical program evolved sequentially rather than as a single fixed protocol. Experiment 1 exposed hosting and interface effects that motivated harmonized API access in Experiments 2 to 3. Experiment 3b followed positional findings from 3a. Experiment 3c followed poor letter consistency in 3b, first for two architectures and then for the full seven-model panel. We read this sequence as an iterative functional analysis: an effect observed in one phase was isolated, a variable was manipulated in the next, and the change in behavior was measured under an otherwise identical schedule. The cost of this approach is that sample sizes and analytic emphasis were shaped by prior results within the same project, and the study should be read as a structured measurement program with sequential extensions rather than as a fully prospective hypothesis battery. Several design constraints bound generalization. All model tasks used Brazilian Portuguese text on a single commercial gateway at one collection window, so results are snapshots of specific model versions rather than stable identities across providers or updates. Temperature was fixed at 1.0 for cross-model comparability even when a provider's default differed (for example, Mistral Large). The human reference is an anonymized archival sample of first completed tournaments on meusvalores.app, not a prospectively recruited comparison cohort matched to the LLM sessions; it supplies distributional benchmarks on the identical task but not paired human-model data. Value-name responding was tested sequentially, first for Mistral Large and DeepSeek V3, then extended exploratorily to the remaining five architectures under the same schedule. All seven exceeded the pair-level semantic null under value-name prompts at T = 1.0, but the extension was not pre-registered separately from the initial two-model follow-up.
Despite Benjamini-Hochberg control at q = .05, contrasts over 48 value labels remain numerous and should be treated as descriptive pattern summaries rather than a closed set of independent confirmations. The translational concern raised in §4.3 was not tested directly in this study: we did not measure whether exposure to a model's value profile changes a user's subsequent value choices, and we therefore do not claim that it does. The present study describes machine behavior under the procedure and analyses the stimulus control that bounds interpretation of that behavior; whether and how that behavior influences human respondents is a separate empirical question that the design does not address.
4.6. Future work
First, mapping model values across multiple procedures. The present study placed seven models under one forced-choice procedure. The same logic extends to other procedures used to study values and preference, such as conditional discrimination tasks, IRAP-style blocks, and relational training and testing, to ask whether the architecture-specific profiles recovered here reproduce under different stimulus arrangements and response channels. A multi-procedure battery would treat model values as a repertoire to be mapped, not a single number to be estimated, and would let the same architectures be compared across procedures in the way that human participants already are.
Second, and most directly motivated by §4.3, a direct test of whether relating a model's value network to a user's network transforms the functions that control the user's subsequent value choices. Designs from the derived-stimulus-relations and equivalence-class literatures are well suited to this: a model's value output is arranged as an antecedent verbal stimulus for a user's subsequent forced-choice responses, and reorganization of the user's choices is measured against a control condition without model exposure. Given that LLMs and conversational agents are already used in ACT and in values-clarification work (Heinz et al., 2025; Reilly et al., 2024; Fujita et al., 2025; Merikko & Räsänen, 2026), this test can be embedded inside ongoing internet-based ACT and AI-assisted values-clarification trials, with model macrotheme profile as a between-conditions variable and valued action as the outcome. The translational concern then becomes a research design rather than a worry.
Third, architecture-specific disclosure as a design variable. The architecture-specific asymmetry means the unit of concern is model-specific configuration, not "AI" in general. Future work should develop and test disclosure formats that present a model's macrotheme profile alongside the user's own hierarchy and evaluate whether disclosure mitigates undue influence on user value choices without undermining the legitimate uses of AI-assisted values work. The practical recommendations of §4.4 (separating profile-level from duel-level reporting, validating the response channel before interpreting side-button duels, not presenting one model run as a stable identity) become design variables whose effects on trust, valued action, and treatment engagement can be measured.
Fourth, replication across the boundary conditions named in §4.5: model versions, providers, and languages; decoding temperature and provider defaults; and idiographic prompts that ask the model to respond as a specific person rather than a generic decision-maker. These are the conditions under which the descriptive claim of §4.1 should, or should not, reproduce. Taken together, the four lines build what the present study points toward: a research program on machine values that runs parallel to work on human values, with shared units and shared procedures, and with the same architectures tested in both.
