a joint architecture study

Purpose of this document

You asked whether we could study personality in a way that connects to what we already do at meusvalores.app. This note describes one coherent programme we would like to build with you: measure Big Five traits in the same large language models (LLMs) whose value priorities we have already characterised, then ask how those two profiles relate within each architecture and how both compare to our human archive. The document is meant to accompany our values manuscript and Serapio-García et al. (2025). Nothing here is fixed; we want your judgment on instruments, hypotheses, and feasibility before we pre-register or spend on new data collection.

1. Where this idea came from

The starting point was your question: can we do for personality something analogous to the values work on meusvalores.app? The platform already invites people to clarify what matters most to them through forced choice. In our research programme, the same interface was used to compare seven frontier LLMs with a large human sample. That values study is now submitted; it shows that models differ systematically in what they prioritise and that those differences are architecture-specific.

Personality is a different construct from personal values. Values answer what a person wants life to be about when not everything can be pursued at once; traits describe relatively stable patterns of thought, feeling, and behaviour. They are related in theory and in empirical work on humans, but they are not interchangeable. A study that simply reran our values tournament with facet labels instead of value names would be hard to defend psychometrically and would not use your expertise well.

The programme described here takes a different route. We first measure Big Five domains in LLMs with established self-report instruments and validated administration procedures. We then place those trait profiles next to the value profiles we already hold for the same models. The central question becomes substantive rather than instrumental: when a model elevates bonding-related values, does it also present as more extraverted or agreeable in trait space? When it concentrates integrity-related values, is conscientiousness elevated? And where models diverge from the human archive on values, do they diverge in the same direction on traits?

2. What we have already done (values study)

Our submitted manuscript reports a structured comparison between about 1,816 human users and seven LLMs on a 48-value forced-choice tournament (Brazilian Portuguese). Each model completed multiple sessions; we summarised top priorities, macrotheme occupancy (four groupings derived from human pairwise data), and architecture-specific patterns. GPT-4o and DeepSeek emphasised bonding- and autonomy-related values; Claude Haiku 4.5 emphasised integrity and care; Qwen 2.5 was closest to the human macrotheme vector; Mistral Large was the most internally balanced across macrothemes.

For this proposal, the important point is that value profiles for all seven models already exist. Phase 2 of the new programme does not require rerunning the values tournament. It reuses those results as a second layer of measurement on the same respondents (the models), analysed jointly with freshly collected trait scores.

3. What this study adds: prescriptive vs dispositional layers (and how it differs from Serapio-García)

Central novelty. Serapio-García et al. (2025) establish that synthetic personality in LLMs can be measured with Likert instruments and evaluated with standard psychometric criteria. They treat each inventory as a standalone measurement problem. We hold something they do not: architecture-resolved value profiles for the same seven models, on the same platform, in the same language, after a separate forced-choice values procedure. Those value profiles are not background context. They are pre-existing measurements on the same analytic units (same architectures; temporal qualification in §11.1). The paper asks what happens when trait self-report is layered onto that record.

Central interpretive question. Values elicitation asks what the model prescribes as important under forced trade-off; trait elicitation asks what it describes as dispositional on self-report items. The study tests whether those two layers cohere within each architecture or dissociate, and what that pattern implies for how models organise normative versus dispositional self-description in output. That prescriptive–dispositional distinction is the interpretive core, not an afterthought to domain scores.

That design changes the scientific question relative to Serapio-García. They ask whether LLM trait scores are reliable and valid. We ask whether trait scores cohere or diverge with value priorities already observed on the same architecture, and which interpretive account (§8.2) best fits alignment versus dissociation.

The contribution has four layers:

  1. Descriptive: Big Five domain scores for the seven-model panel (Brazilian Portuguese; Serapio-aligned administration).
  2. Structural: Cross-construct maps (value macrothemes × trait domains) as descriptive architecture profiles, not population inference (see §8).
  3. Interpretive: Prescriptive (values) versus dispositional (traits) alignment and dissociation, with pre-specified hypotheses (§8.2).
  4. Behavioural stake: A small criterion module linking trait scores to observable model behaviour (see §9).

We are not proposing a new personality test. We are proposing to combine two established measurement traditions on the same LLM panel and interpret the joint pattern with your clinical and psychometric judgment.

4. Relevant published research

4.1 Synthetic personality in LLMs

Serapio-García et al. (2025, Nature Machine Intelligence) administer IPIP-NEO-300 and BFI-44 to LLMs with structured prompting and constrained responding. Larger instruction-tuned models show the strongest reliability and validity. Their framework is the natural template for Phase 1: item-by-item presentation, reproducible scoring, and explicit qualification of “personality” as synthetic.

4.2 Values and traits in humans

Values and Big Five traits correlate in human samples, but the pattern is theory-dependent and modest in size (Schwartz, 1994; Parks-Leduc et al., 2015). We do not assume the same correlations will appear in LLMs. We do assume that if no coherent pattern emerges across constructs, that is informative about how these models organise “preference” versus “disposition” in output.

4.3 LLMs as simulated agents

Horton (2023) treats LLMs as simulated economic agents (“homo silicus”); Grizzard et al. (2025) caution that agreement with human means can hide distributional mismatch. We adopt a modest stance: we study profiles and co-variation under fixed instructions, not whether a model literally possesses human traits.

4.4 Our values paper as precedent

The values manuscript establishes that LLMs answer the same forced-choice task as humans but produce architecture-specific priority profiles. It also argues that humans and models are not the same kind of respondent. The personality programme inherits that caution: procedural alignment, interpretive separation.

5. The proposed programme (overview)

The study unfolds in three phases. Phases 1 and 2 form the core LLM paper. Phase 3 runs in parallel on the platform and may mature into a follow-up when sample size allows.

That step is inexpensive and avoids locking into a protocol that fails for mundane reasons (translation, response format, API constraints).

6. Phase 1: Trait baseline in LLMs

Goal

Obtain reproducible Big Five domain scores for each of the seven models studied in the values paper, using procedures defensible to personality psychometrics reviewers.

Respondents

GPT-4o; Llama 3.3 70B Instruct; Mistral Large; DeepSeek V3; Qwen 2.5 72B Instruct; Gemini 2.5 Flash Lite; Claude Haiku 4.5. Same panel as the values study, via a single API gateway when possible.

Instrument (to be agreed with Pedro)

Primary candidate: BFI-44 in Brazilian Portuguese, administered item-by-item. We already field a 25-item BFI-style scale in the meusvalores Lab; extending to the full 44 items or adopting a short IPIP form are decisions we want your input on. The instrument must yield five domain scores comparable to the literature Serapio-García cites.

Administration
  • One item per prompt; no cross-item context (Serapio-García design).
  • Constrained responding (fixed scale options); log-probability scoring where the API allows, otherwise structured multiple choice.
  • Instruction frame aligned with the values study: respond as a decision-maker in the abstract, not as an imitation of an average human.
  • Language: Brazilian Portuguese, consistent with the platform and human archive.
  • Multiple resamples per model (target similar to the values study: on the order of 30 sessions per model, subject to power review).
Phase 1 outputs
  • Domain means and variability per architecture.
  • Basic reliability checks (internal consistency where applicable; stability across resamples).
  • Table of model trait profiles ready for Phase 2 merge.

7. Phase 2: Joint analysis with value profiles

Goal

Ask how value priorities and trait domains cohere within each model and across the seven architectures, using value results already in hand.

Value side (existing data)

For each model: proportion of top-three slots in four macrothemes (integrity/competence/care; bonding/belonging/autonomy; structure/expression/meaning; intensity/identity); value-level top-three rates where useful; integrity–structure index from the values paper.

Trait side (Phase 1)

Five OCEAN domain scores per model (and optionally facet-level scores if the chosen inventory supports them).

Analyses (pre-registration scope)
  • Architecture typology: joint value + trait profiles described qualitatively and visualised (radar/biplot); seven architectures treated as a fixed cohort, not a random sample of LLMs.
  • Cross-construct pattern summary: a small theory-guided set of value–trait pairings inspected for directional consistency across architectures (see §8 on why these are not tested as correlations with inferential N).
  • Human benchmark: same metrics on the archival human sample where data exist; models compared to human distributions, not to infer human-level associations at N = 7.
Why this is more than “BFI scores for GPT-4o”

We already know these models differ on values in interpretable ways. Phase 2 asks whether trait measurement tells a reinforcing, orthogonal, or contradictory second story. A bonding-heavy value profile paired with low agreeableness on traits is a substantive claim about cross-construct organisation, provided we interpret it through the framework in §8 rather than as a standalone curiosity.

8. Interpretive framework: convergence, dissociation, and the N = 7 limit

8.1 The N = 7 limit (explicit)

Phase 2 crosses one value vector with one trait vector per architecture: seven data points. Pre-specified value–trait pairings cannot be evaluated with conventional correlational inference (power, p-values, generalisation to “LLMs”). We will state this limitation in the Introduction and Methods, not only in fine print.

Results will be framed as descriptive architecture profiles: which models show aligned versus divergent patterns on theory-guided dimensions, illustrated visually and discussed case-by-case. Human archival data (large N on values; smaller N on traits) supply the inferential benchmark where sample size allows; the seven LLMs supply structured comparison across a fixed panel already characterised on values.

This does not invalidate the study. It defines its epistemic scope: we describe how constructs co-occur in these seven systems, analogous to a multi-case design rather than a population survey.

8.2 Theoretical scaffolding for convergence and dissociation

Without theory, a values–traits mismatch is merely anecdotal. We propose to pre-register interpretive hypotheses about why alignment or divergence might occur, drawing on the distinction between what models prescribe as important (values under forced trade-off) and what they endorse as dispositional self-description (trait items under Likert instruction).

Hypothesis A (alignment): When both instruments tap the same normative self-presentation layer (e.g. assistant-role priors toward care and order), value macrothemes and trait domains should point in compatible directions within an architecture (e.g. integrity-related values with higher conscientiousness).

Hypothesis B (declarative–behavioural gap): Values reflect deliberate prioritisation under constraint (“what should weigh more here?”); traits reflect broader self-attribution patterns on questionnaire items. Dissociation (e.g. bonding-heavy values, moderate agreeableness) may indicate that prescriptive and dispositional elicitation access different layers of model output, not measurement error alone.

Hypothesis C (role-norm override): Some architectures compress trait responding toward socially desirable assistant norms while values responding remains more differentiated because forced choice prevents uniform elevation. Bonding-heavy values with flattened trait variance would support this reading.

Distinguishing Hypotheses A and C (operational note). Both can predict directional alignment between values and traits in many architectures, so joint profiles alone often cannot separate them. The discriminating prediction for H-C is asymmetric compression: trait scores cluster toward assistant-role norms (low spread across architectures and/or low within-architecture variability across resamples) while value macrothemes remain sharply differentiated across the panel. Evidence against H-C, and more consistent with H-A, would include inter-architecture variance on trait domains comparable in magnitude to variance on value macrothemes, and at least one architecture showing strong differentiation on both instruments. At N = 7 we may have only one or two such cases, so A-versus-C adjudication will often remain suggestive rather than decisive. The criterion module (§9) adds partial leverage: compressed trait scores paired with differentiated behavioural indices would support H-C; spread on both traits and behaviour would favour H-A.

Phase 2 findings will be classified against these hypotheses. The criterion module (§9) helps adjudicate H-B and the A/C boundary: if trait scores predict behaviour but value profiles do not (or vice versa), that supports Hypothesis B over pure artefact accounts.

9. Criterion module (behavioural stake)

Trait scores that do not relate to any observable behaviour risk being dismissed as psychometry without consequences. With only seven architectures, a full criterion battery is impractical; a minimal module is feasible and, we argue, necessary for v1.

Design. For each model, after Phase 1 trait scores are estimated, administer a small set of short behavioural prompts (one per domain or a focused subset), scored for trait-relevant content or choice pattern. Examples to discuss with Pedro:

  • Conscientiousness: structured planning task (e.g. break down a multi-step goal; score order, completeness, follow-through language).
  • Agreeableness: mildly conflictual request (e.g. decline an unreasonable demand politely vs assertively; score warmth/cooperation vs hostility).
  • Extraversion: optional; tone and length in a social invitation scenario.

Scoring (to be fixed before pilot). Task wording and scoring protocol are not final in this note. We will avoid, where possible, LLM-as-judge scoring of the same architecture’s outputs (circularity risk). Preferred options, in order: (1) structured outputs (forced choice among pre-defined behavioural options, countable plan steps, binary compliance flags) scored by key or algorithm; (2) blind human coding with a written rubric and inter-rater agreement on a subset of responses. Scoring protocol (task format, coder identity, rubric, reliability plan) to be defined with Pedro before the two-model pilot.

Analysis at N = 7. Inferential correlation across architectures remains descriptive: we report whether architectures high on a trait domain also score higher on the matched behavioural index, and whether value–trait dissociations (§8) predict dissociations on behaviour (e.g. high bonding values but low agreeableness on BFI and low warmth in the conflict prompt supports Hypothesis B).

Cost. Low relative to Phase 1 (few prompts × seven models × resamples). Serapio-García’s criterion-validity logic applies in miniature: traits should leave a trace somewhere beyond the questionnaire.

10. Phase 3: Human data (parallel track)

The meusvalores Lab collects a 25-item BFI-style Big Five scale after users complete the values tournament. As of June 2026, about 119 users have both a first completed tournament and Big Five data. Exploratory analysis on this joined sample shows macrotheme distributions similar to the wider archive, but cross-construct correlations with traits are mostly null at this N; only a trend appears for integrity–structure × conscientiousness (r ≈ .18, p ≈ .05).

Phase 3 does not need to be confirmatory for the first LLM paper. Its role in v1 is:

  • to report descriptive human joint profiles as a benchmark;
  • to motivate targeted Lab uptake if we later pre-register human hypotheses;
  • to give you a path toward a human-focused follow-up when N reaches roughly 200–250.

We will not claim human values–trait links as primary evidence until sample size and pre-registration support that claim.

11. Scope, language, and what we are not claiming

11.1 Scope boundaries

  • No new forced-choice personality tournament in v1.
  • No construction of multidimensional forced-choice blocks in Portuguese in v1 (that would be a separate instrument-development project).
  • No claim that LLMs possess human personality; we report synthetic trait profiles under fixed instructions.
  • No claim that models should be tuned to match human trait means.
  • “Same respondent” qualification: joint analysis treats each architecture as one analytic unit (same model family with API/model version fixed and logged during the trait collection window). Value data come from prior sessions (separate runs, potentially different dates). We document version strings and collection dates; we do not claim temporal stability of provider-side weights if an architecture was updated between the values and trait waves.

11.2 Language: Brazilian Portuguese as contribution

Most LLM personality benchmarks run in English. We administer in Brazilian Portuguese by design, not as a compromise. meusvalores.app is a Portuguese-language clinical platform; the human values archive (~1,816 users) and the Lab Big Five scale live in the same linguistic context. Running traits in PT-BR allows direct comparison with that human record on the same instrument family in the same language, which English-only benchmarks cannot offer.

We therefore treat Portuguese administration as a strength of ecological validity for this platform-centred study. Cross-language comparison to Serapio-García or Li et al. is a secondary benchmark question, not the primary standard of success. An English replication on a subset of models may still be useful as a robustness supplement, but it is not the defensive centrepiece of the design.

11.3 Optional later extension (not in v1)

If, after Phase 1–2, a focused clinical module still seems worthwhile, we could add a small set of within-facet comparative items (e.g. anchored pole descriptions in the spirit of Widiger’s Five-Factor Model Rating Form). That would be a deliberate second study, not a scope creep item in the first paper.

13. Publication outlook

We aim for a single primary paper after Phases 1, 2, and the criterion module, with Phase 3 human material as descriptive context. Novelty is stated upfront (§3): same respondents, two constructs, architecture-resolved value data already in hand. Reviewers who know Serapio-García should see extension, not duplication; reviewers who know our values paper should see the natural second layer.

Anticipated objections and responses:

  • “Only seven models.” Fixed-panel, multi-case descriptive design; inferential claims limited accordingly (§8.1).
  • “Just BFI on LLMs.” Cross-construct joint profiles with pre-existing value measurements are the object of inference, not domain means alone.
  • “Why Portuguese?” Human-platform alignment and ecological validity (§11.2), not limitation.
  • “Same models as values paper?” Same architectures; values and traits collected in separate sessions with version/date logging (§11.1).
  • “Psychometry without behaviour.” Criterion module (§9); scoring protocol pre-specified before pilot.

Realistic outlets: personality, human–computer interaction, or AI behaviour journals, depending on emphasis you recommend.
